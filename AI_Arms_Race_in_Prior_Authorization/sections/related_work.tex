\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

AI and machine learning are being deployed on both sides of healthcare administration. On the insurer side, a scoping review identifies eight application domains across health insurance, including claims processing, fraud detection, utilization monitoring, and policy decision support \citep{ramezani2025ai}. On the provider side, \citet{owolabi2025ml} show that elastic net classifiers can predict which hospital admission denials will overturn on appeal with 84\% accuracy; post-deployment, monthly appeal volumes and overturned denials nearly doubled. These systems each optimize for one party independently; neither models the interaction dynamics that emerge when both sides deploy AI simultaneously.

Multi-agent LLM simulation offers a methodology for studying such interactions. \citet{fan2025aihospital} introduce AI Hospital, where doctor, patient, examiner, and chief physician agents interact across multi-turn diagnostic conversations. Their work demonstrates that LLM diagnostic performance degrades substantially in interactive settings relative to static benchmarks (even GPT-4 achieves less than half the upper-bound score) and that a multi-doctor dispute resolution mechanism can partially recover accuracy. This establishes that multi-agent simulation surfaces emergent behaviors not visible in single-agent evaluation.

Our work connects these threads. The single-agent systems above optimize for one side of the PA transaction; we model both sides simultaneously within a shared encounter, making the interaction itself the object of study. Unlike collaborative multi-agent settings where agents share a diagnostic goal, our agents hold opposing objectives (the provider maximizes approved services while the insurer minimizes unnecessary coverage) and operate under information asymmetry, each receiving a different document (clinical guideline vs.\ coverage policy). We further introduce strategy as an experimental variable via prompt injection, enabling controlled comparison of cooperative and adversarial postures within the same clinical scenario.
