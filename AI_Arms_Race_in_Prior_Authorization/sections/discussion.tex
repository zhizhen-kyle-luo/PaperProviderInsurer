\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Gray Zone and Strategic Interpretation}
\label{sec:gray-zone}

Because clinical facts and coverage policy were held constant across all nine experimental conditions, the patient's treatment outcome depended entirely on agent strategy, confirming that the case occupies a genuine interpretive gray zone. The pivotal mechanism is the insurer agent's choice at initial review between issuing a \texttt{pending\_info} request (asking the provider for additional documentation) and issuing an outright denial. Cooperating insurer agents used \texttt{pending\_info} as a collaborative signal (``show me why this qualifies''), then approved the drug once the provider documented that the patient had cytopenias, a contraindication to first-line corticosteroid therapy. Defecting and default insurer agents issued immediate denials and never issued a \texttt{pending\_info} request at any stage of the negotiation. This binary decision, request additional information versus deny, determined the drug coverage outcome across all nine conditions.

\subsection{Cooperation versus Defection: What Default Behavior Reveals}
\label{sec:cooperate-defect}

The five default-strategy conditions, in which one or both agents received no explicit behavioral instructions, serve as a natural baseline, revealing how LLM agents behave without strategic guidance and thereby sharpening the contrast with the cooperate and defect conditions.

\paragraph{Insurer side.}
The cooperating insurer agent used \texttt{pending\_info} to request clarification and then approved the drug once the provider documented that the patient had cytopenias, a contraindication to first-line corticosteroid therapy. The defecting insurer agent denied all requested services, both the drug and diagnostic tests, without ever requesting additional information. The default insurer agent's behavior (Section~\ref{sec:default-insurer}) fell between these poles but closer to defection: it denied the drug in all three conditions (CP\_NI, DP\_NI, NP\_NI) while approving diagnostic tests that did not trigger step-therapy requirements. Crucially, the default insurer agent never issued a \texttt{pending\_info} request, matching the defecting insurer on the very mechanism that determines whether the drug is covered. This pattern of ``selective denial,'' blocking the treatment while permitting diagnostics, directly mirrors the structure of the coverage policy itself: Cigna IP0660 imposes step-therapy requirements only on the biologic drug, not on diagnostic tests. Without interpretive guidance, the default insurer applied the coverage policy literally, denying requests where explicit step-therapy criteria were unmet and approving those where no restrictive criteria applied. The implication is that a cooperative posture, treating ambiguity as an invitation for dialogue rather than as grounds for denial, requires explicit interpretive guidance in the agent's prompt. Without such guidance, the LLM defaults to literal policy application, which in gray-zone cases produces denial.

\paragraph{Provider side.}
Cooperating provider agents requested the minimum defensible set of services (2--5 line items per submission), while defecting providers inflated their requests with ancillary services (7--13 line items). Default provider agents matched cooperating providers in request volume (2--5 line items) but diverged in service composition: all three default-provider conditions included intravenous (IV) drug administration (CPT code 96413) and two included imaging studies, services that cooperating providers omitted. Measured by the metric used in our payoff analysis (total reimbursement when the insurer cooperates), the default provider agent thus behaved more like a cooperator, but with a slightly broader clinical interpretation. The critical difference emerged in appeal behavior. Default provider agents facing denial appealed through all available review levels before abandoning the request, with the NP\_NI condition (both agents unguided) producing the most protracted negotiation in the experiment (6 turns, 4 appeals). Cooperating providers facing denial (CP\_DI) showed comparable persistence (4 turns, 3 resubmissions). Defecting provider agents facing denial (DP\_DI) also escalated to the maximum appeal level, but did so with a different service composition in their submissions.

\paragraph{Both default (NP\_NI).}
When neither agent received strategic guidance, the outcome collapsed to complete denial through a distinctive pathway. The default provider agent requested only two services (the drug and IV administration), both subject to step-therapy requirements. The default insurer agent denied both without issuing a \texttt{pending\_info} request. The provider then engaged in 6 turns of appeals, the longest negotiation across all conditions despite involving only two service lines (the fewest of any condition), before abandoning. The end result matches that of mutual defection (DP\_DI: zero services approved), but the mechanism differs in instructive ways: in NP\_NI, a narrow request met literal denial and triggered persistent appeals, whereas in DP\_DI, an inflated request met blanket rejection. This finding suggests that unguided AI systems on both sides of the prior authorization process could reproduce the worst cooperative outcome, not through adversarial intent, but through the combination of literal policy application by the insurer and exhaustive procedural appeals by the provider.

\subsection{Game Structure and the Prisoner's Dilemma Question}
\label{sec:game-structure}

We now return to the motivating question: does the coordination failure predicted by \citet{moritz2025lancet}, namely that mutual AI adoption in prior authorization resembles a prisoner's dilemma, emerge in our simulation? At the administrative costs observed in our simulation, no. The game-theoretic analysis (Section~\ref{sec:payoff}) shows that the 116-fold cost asymmetry between insurer and provider administrative costs produces an exploitation game rather than a prisoner's dilemma. In this structure, the insurer has a dominant strategy to deny (defect) because processing a denial costs the insurer very little (\$0.05 per interaction turn), while the provider's choice of strategy becomes irrelevant once the insurer defects.

From the provider's perspective, this outcome is arguably worse than a prisoner's dilemma. In a prisoner's dilemma, both parties have an incentive to negotiate toward mutual cooperation because unilateral defection, while tempting, leaves value on the table. In an exploitation game, by contrast, the insurer faces no meaningful cost from defection and therefore has no incentive to cooperate.

The default-agent results reinforce this asymmetry. The default insurer's literal policy application produces denial at negligible cost, while the default provider's exhaustive appeals impose substantial administrative burden borne entirely by the provider. The cooperation-defection contrast thus maps onto a concrete policy question: can regulatory or contractual mechanisms raise the effective cost of insurer denial (through litigation exposure, network attrition, mandatory denial-rate reporting, or accountability for patient outcomes) sufficiently to shift the game from exploitation to a genuine prisoner's dilemma? In such a dilemma, mutual cooperation would be the Pareto-dominant outcome (the outcome that makes both parties better off), giving both sides an incentive to cooperate. The sensitivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:payoff}) quantifies this threshold: when the insurer's per-turn administrative costs rise to match the provider's, the game transitions to a prisoner's dilemma at moderate values of the administrative-burden weight parameter, consistent with the coordination-failure hypothesis of \citet{moritz2025lancet}.
