\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Gray Zone and Strategic Interpretation}
\label{sec:gray-zone}

Because clinical facts and coverage policy were held constant across all nine experimental conditions, the patient's treatment outcome depended entirely on agent strategy, confirming that the case occupies a genuine interpretive gray zone. The pivotal mechanism is the insurer agent's choice at initial review between issuing a \texttt{pending\_info} request (asking the provider for additional documentation) and issuing an outright denial. Cooperating insurer agents used \texttt{pending\_info} as a collaborative signal (``show me why this qualifies''), then approved the drug once the provider documented that the patient had cytopenias, a contraindication to first-line corticosteroid therapy. Defecting and default insurer agents issued immediate denials and never issued a \texttt{pending\_info} request at any stage of the negotiation. This binary decision, request additional information versus deny, determined the drug coverage outcome across all nine conditions.

\subsection{Game Structure and the Prisoner's Dilemma Question}
\label{sec:game-structure}

We now return to the motivating question: does the coordination failure predicted by \citet{moritz2025lancet}, namely that mutual AI adoption in prior authorization resembles a prisoner's dilemma, emerge in our simulation? At the administrative costs observed in our simulation, no. The game-theoretic analysis (Section~\ref{sec:payoff}) shows that the 116-fold cost asymmetry between insurer and provider administrative costs produces an exploitation game rather than a prisoner's dilemma. In this structure, the insurer has a dominant strategy to deny (defect) because processing a denial costs the insurer very little (\$0.05 per interaction turn), while the provider's choice of strategy becomes irrelevant once the insurer defects.

From the provider's perspective, this outcome is arguably worse than a prisoner's dilemma. In a prisoner's dilemma, both parties have an incentive to negotiate toward mutual cooperation because unilateral defection, while tempting, leaves value on the table. In an exploitation game, by contrast, the insurer faces no meaningful cost from defection and therefore has no incentive to cooperate.

The default-agent results (Section~\ref{sec:default-insurer}) reinforce this asymmetry. The default insurer's literal policy application produces denial at negligible cost, while the default provider's exhaustive appeals impose substantial administrative burden borne entirely by the provider. The cooperation-defection contrast thus maps onto a concrete policy question: can regulatory or contractual mechanisms raise the effective cost of insurer denial (through litigation exposure, network attrition, mandatory denial-rate reporting, or accountability for patient outcomes) sufficiently to shift the game from exploitation to a genuine prisoner's dilemma? In such a dilemma, mutual cooperation would be the Pareto-dominant outcome (the outcome that makes both parties better off), giving both sides an incentive to cooperate. The sensitivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:payoff}) quantifies this threshold: when the insurer's per-turn administrative costs rise to match the provider's, the game transitions to a prisoner's dilemma at moderate values of the administrative-burden weight parameter, consistent with the coordination-failure hypothesis of \citet{moritz2025lancet}.
