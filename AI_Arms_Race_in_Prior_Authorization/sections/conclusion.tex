\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a multi-agent simulation in which LLM-driven provider and insurer agents negotiate prior authorization under information asymmetry. Three findings emerge from the 3$\times$3 factorial experiment on a clinically ambiguous case:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Ambiguity is resolved by a single insurer decision.} Whether the insurer requests additional documentation or denies outright determines the drug coverage outcome across all nine conditions. The mechanism of coordination failure is not adversarial strategy but the absence of interpretive flexibility.

    \item \textbf{Unguided AI defaults to denial.} Default insurer agents, given no strategic instructions, apply coverage policy literally and deny ambiguous treatments---even when the same clinical evidence would support approval under a good-faith reading. When both agents lack guidance (NP\_NI), the interaction collapses to the same deadlock as mutual defection, through bureaucratic attrition rather than adversarial intent.

    \item \textbf{The game's strategic structure is parameter-dependent.} At directly observed administrative costs, the 116-fold cost asymmetry makes denial nearly free for the insurer, producing an exploitation game. Under richer cost models that incorporate litigation risk, reputational damage, and network-exit costs---factors present in practice but not captured by the \$0.05 CAQH transaction cost---the sensitivity analysis shows the game transitions to a prisoner's dilemma where mutual cooperation is Pareto-dominant yet both sides face incentives to defect.
\end{enumerate}

These findings suggest that policy interventions aimed at raising the effective cost of insurer denial---through mandatory denial-rate reporting, litigation exposure, or accountability for patient outcomes---could shift the strategic structure from one-sided exploitation toward a regime where both parties benefit from cooperation. The sensitivity analysis provides quantitative thresholds for this transition. Future work should extend the framework to multiple clinical cases, coverage policies, foundation models, and replicated runs to assess generalizability.
